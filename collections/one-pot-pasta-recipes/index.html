One Pot Pasta Recipes

Fourteen tested one pot pasta recipes — from creamy Tuscan chicken to vodka rigatoni, cacio e pepe, and shrimp scampi. All cook start-to-finish in one pan.

One-pot pasta isn't just a gimmick to reduce washing up — when it's done right, it produces a silkier sauce than the traditional "cook pasta separately, toss with sauce" method. The starch that would normally go down the drain stays in the pan, and the pasta finishes cooking in the sauce itself, which emulsifies everything into a glossy, restaurant-style coating. The trick is getting the liquid ratio right.

The fourteen recipes in this collection have all been tested to hit the same end point: cooked-through pasta, a sauce that clings to every piece, and no sticky starchy residue in the pan. They range from five-ingredient weeknight saves (cacio e pepe, spinach ricotta) to full Sunday-dinner builds (vodka rigatoni, creamy sausage tortellini). Pan size and liquid ratios are calibrated for a standard 10–12 inch sauté pan or a 4–5 quart Dutch oven.

The liquid-to-pasta ratio that actually works

The biggest mistake in one-pot pasta is using the same water volume you'd use when boiling pasta separately (huge amounts). For a one-pot dish, the ratio is: 3–3.5 cups of liquid per 450 g of dried pasta. That's enough to submerge the pasta during cooking and produce a sauce you can spoon, not a soup. Use too much liquid and you end up boiling off water for 20 minutes and overcooking the pasta. Use too little and the bottom scorches before the pasta is tender.

What counts as liquid matters, too. Broth contributes salt and flavor; water contributes neither but dilutes less. Wine contributes acidity but boils off. Tomato sauce counts as liquid but drastically reduces as it thickens. Cream and milk count but tend to scorch if they're added too early — add dairy in the last 5 minutes. A good working ratio for most recipes is 2 cups broth + 1 cup water + 0.5 cup wine or additional liquid.

Pasta shape matters more than it seems

  • Short shapes (penne, rigatoni, rotini, farfalle): most forgiving — they cook evenly in one pot and hold sauce well.
  • Long strands (linguine, spaghetti, fettuccine): need to be snapped in half or broken up after 2 minutes of cooking so they submerge. The exception is large pans where long strands lay flat.
  • Orzo and small shapes: cook fastest (6–8 minutes) and absorb liquid most aggressively — use slightly less liquid than the standard ratio.
  • Fresh pasta and tortellini: dramatically shorter cook (3–5 minutes). Add at the end after the main sauce is established.
  • Whole wheat and gluten-free: absorb more liquid — add 15 % more broth than the recipe calls for.

When to stir and when to leave alone

One-pot pasta benefits from gentle attention, not constant stirring. Stir thoroughly at the start to distribute the pasta evenly and prevent sticking to the bottom. Then let it cook undisturbed for 3–4 minutes — this is when the starch from the pasta releases into the liquid and begins to thicken the sauce. Over-stirring breaks up the pasta and turns the starch into a gummy paste instead of a silk. Stir every 2–3 minutes after the initial period, just enough to prevent the bottom from catching.

Finishing with fat and acid

Every great pasta dish, one-pot or otherwise, ends with two finishing additions that are often skipped. First: a fat — butter, olive oil, or grated hard cheese (parmesan, pecorino, grana padano) stirred in off the heat. This emulsifies the remaining liquid into a glossy sauce. Second: an acid — a squeeze of lemon, a splash of vinegar, or in tomato dishes a little more wine — to brighten what can otherwise become one-note heavy. The amount is small (1 tablespoon is usually enough) but the difference in flavor is significant.

The fourteen recipes below each note the exact liquid ratio, stirring schedule, and finishing touches. Start with cacio e pepe or the spinach ricotta if you're new to one-pot pasta — they're the most forgiving and teach the technique in about 15 minutes.

Frequently asked questions

Why does my one-pot pasta come out gummy or sticky?
Two usual causes: too much pasta relative to liquid (leaving the starch nowhere to go), or over-stirring (which mechanically breaks down the starch into a paste). Stick closer to 3 cups of liquid per 450 g of dried pasta, and stir only every 2–3 minutes rather than constantly. If you're already gummy at serving time, a splash of hot broth stirred in loosens the sauce to the right consistency.
Can I use whole wheat or gluten-free pasta in one-pot recipes?
Yes, but both absorb noticeably more liquid than regular wheat pasta. Add about 15 % more broth than the recipe calls for. Whole wheat also tolerates overcooking better; gluten-free can go from al dente to mush in 60 seconds, so check often in the final 2 minutes. Start tasting at 2 minutes before the package's stated time.
Do I need to break long pasta like spaghetti for a one-pot recipe?
In most pots, yes — break the strands in half so they fit submerged in the liquid. The alternative is to add the strands whole, cover briefly with a lid for 2 minutes to soften, then stir them down into the liquid once they're pliable. Either works; breaking is faster and more reliable.
Can one-pot pasta be made ahead and reheated?
Yes, but it needs loosening on reheat. Cooked pasta continues to absorb sauce as it sits, turning dense and sometimes dry by the next day. Add 60–100 ml of broth or milk (depending on whether the sauce is dairy-based) when reheating and stir over medium-low heat until it comes back to a saucy consistency. Avoid microwave reheating for creamy sauces — they break more reliably on the stove.
Why does my creamy one-pot pasta sauce break or curdle?
Almost always from adding dairy too early or at too high a heat. Add cream, milk, or cream cheese only in the last 4–5 minutes of cooking, with the heat turned down to medium-low. High-acid sauces (tomato, wine) curdle dairy especially fast — temper the dairy by stirring 2–3 tablespoons of hot sauce into it first, then add the warmed mixture back to the pot.
What size pot is best for one-pot pasta?
A 10–12 inch sauté pan with straight sides, or a 4–5 quart Dutch oven. Wider is better than deeper for short pasta shapes — more surface area for even cooking and faster evaporation. Deep, narrow pots concentrate heat at the bottom and scorch. For four servings of 450 g pasta, a 12-inch pan is ideal.
