One Pot Chicken Recipes

Ten tested one pot chicken recipes: Tuscan chicken pasta, butter chicken, chicken tortilla soup, teriyaki chicken rice, and more — all in a single pan or pot.

Chicken is the most versatile weeknight protein we cook with, and every one of these ten recipes proves that a one-pot approach doesn't mean compromise. Done well, cooking chicken in the same pan as its sauce and starch concentrates flavors in a way separate cooking can't: the fond from searing the meat dissolves into the braising liquid, the rice or pasta absorbs the chicken's rendered fat, and the whole thing develops the depth of a long braise in 30–40 minutes.

The ten recipes below span comfort food (Tuscan chicken pasta, butter chicken, honey garlic chicken rice), lighter options (lemon orzo, pesto chicken, chicken burrito bowl), and pure weeknight shortcuts (teriyaki chicken rice, chicken tortilla soup). Pan sizes are 10–12 inches sauté, with options noted for Dutch ovens where depth matters. Chicken cuts are specified recipe-by-recipe — using the wrong cut is the biggest single cause of one-pot disappointment.

Thighs vs. breasts: it matters more than you think

Boneless skinless thighs are almost always the better choice for one-pot chicken. They don't dry out during the long simmer that rice and pasta require, they contribute more flavor to the surrounding liquid, and they hold up to reheating (whereas breast meat turns chalky on day two). Use breasts only when the recipe is fast (under 20 minutes) or when you're specifically looking for lean white meat. For anything that includes the words "simmer," "braise," or "add broth and cook until rice is tender," use thighs.

That said, boneless breasts have one genuine advantage: quicker cooking, so they work well in the pasta-based recipes here where the chicken sears first, comes out of the pan, and returns at the end for 2–3 minutes to heat through.

Sear first, even when it's inconvenient

Browning is flavor, not decoration. The single biggest quality difference between a so-so one-pot chicken dinner and a great one is whether the chicken got a real sear before the liquid went in. Season the chicken, pat dry, and let it hit a hot pan (medium-high) in oil until one side is deeply golden — typically 3–4 minutes without moving. Flip, give the second side 1–2 minutes, and then remove the chicken to finish the aromatics and starch. Return the chicken to the pan when instructed to reheat through and finish cooking.

The fond left in the pan — those brown bits stuck to the bottom — is where the dish's foundational flavor comes from. Add your onion or garlic to that same pan, let them sweat, then deglaze with wine, broth, or tomato sauce. Scrape the fond up as the liquid comes to a simmer. Everything that happens next is built on that base.

The liquid and starch balance

  • Rice: 1 cup dry rice + 2 cups liquid, cooked covered 18–20 minutes.
  • Pasta: 450 g dry pasta + 3–3.5 cups liquid, uncovered, 10–12 minutes with occasional stirring.
  • Orzo: 1 cup orzo + 2.25–2.5 cups liquid, stirred once halfway through a 10–12 minute simmer.
  • Quinoa: 1 cup + 1.75 cups liquid, covered, 14–16 minutes.
  • Beans and broth (soups): aim for enough liquid to float the ingredients with 2 cm of broth on top.

Finishing touches that change everything

Every one of these ten recipes ends with at least one of three finishing moves: fresh herbs stirred in off the heat (parsley, cilantro, basil); a squeeze of acid (lemon, lime, or a splash of vinegar); or a fat emulsion (butter, cream, or a hard grated cheese). These last-minute additions take 30 seconds but noticeably lift flavors that can otherwise become heavy or flat during a long simmer. Don't skip them — the recipes are calibrated assuming they're included.

Start with the creamy Tuscan chicken pasta if you're new to one-pot cooking — it teaches searing, deglazing, and liquid-starch balance in a single 30-minute dish. From there, the rice-based recipes (chicken rice, teriyaki, honey garlic) are the most forgiving for weeknight timing.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use chicken breasts instead of thighs in one-pot recipes?
For short cooks (under 20 minutes), yes. For anything with a long simmer — rice-based recipes, braises, stews — thighs give significantly better results. Breasts dry out at simmering temperatures over about 15 minutes. If you strongly prefer breasts, sear them, remove them from the pan, and return them only for the final 2–3 minutes.
How do I know when one-pot chicken and rice is done?
Two checks: the rice should be tender with no visible liquid pooling when you tilt the pan, and the chicken internal temperature should be at least 74 °C (165 °F) in the thickest part. If the rice is done but the chicken isn't, cover and give it another 3–4 minutes off the heat — residual cooking finishes the chicken without overcooking the rice.
Can I freeze one-pot chicken dinners?
Yes, with caveats. Rice-based and brothy recipes freeze well for up to 2 months. Creamy recipes (Alfredo, Tuscan pasta) can separate on reheat — the dairy breaks. If freezing a creamy recipe, leave out the dairy, freeze the sauce base, and stir in cream or cheese fresh on reheat. Thaw overnight in the fridge before reheating.
Why is the bottom of my pan burning during one-pot cooking?
Usually heat too high or liquid too low. Once rice or pasta begins absorbing liquid, drop the heat to low and cover (for rice) or keep on a bare simmer and stir (for pasta). A heavy-bottomed pan — cast iron, enameled Dutch oven, multi-ply stainless — dramatically reduces scorching compared to a thin pan.
Can I cook brown rice in a one-pot chicken recipe?
Yes, but extend the cook time significantly: brown rice needs about 40–45 minutes versus 18–20 for white rice, and slightly more liquid (about 2.25 cups per cup of rice). The chicken will overcook in that time, so add chicken pieces in the last 15 minutes rather than at the start, or use bone-in thighs that tolerate the longer simmer.
What size pan works best for one-pot chicken recipes?
For 4 servings, a 10–12 inch sauté pan with a lid (for rice-based dishes) or a 4–5 quart Dutch oven (for braises and soups). Wider pans work better for pasta (more surface area, faster evaporation). Narrower pots work better for rice (better heat retention, less stirring).
