One Pot Soup Recipes

Ten tested one pot soup recipes: coconut lentil, Tuscan white bean, minestrone, chicken tortilla, taco soup, lasagna soup, and more. All from pantry staples.

Soups are the original one-pot meal, and the ten in this collection are the ones we return to every week of the colder months. Unlike pasta or rice-based one-pot dishes, soups reward longer cooking and forgive ratio mistakes — add a little extra broth and you have a thinner soup, reduce it longer and you have a stew. That flexibility makes them ideal for weeknight cooking when you don't want to measure anything precisely.

What separates these ten from endless online variations is that they're all built around pantry-plus-fresh structures: dried beans or canned chickpeas as the base protein, a hardy aromatic foundation (onion, garlic, carrot, celery), a tomato or coconut milk body, and a small handful of fresh vegetables or herbs added at the end. None of them require obscure ingredients. All of them improve the next day — these are the Sunday-cook-once, eat-three-days recipes.

The foundation every great soup shares

Build flavor before adding liquid. The difference between a soup that tastes like salted broth and one with actual depth is whether you took five extra minutes to brown the aromatics. Sweat onions, carrots, and celery in a tablespoon of oil with a big pinch of salt over medium heat until they're visibly softened and just starting to color at the edges — 8–10 minutes. Then add garlic for 60 seconds, then spices for 30 seconds (this "blooms" the spices in the oil and transforms their flavor), then tomato paste if the recipe uses it (another 2 minutes of stirring develops it from raw to caramelized). Only then add your liquid.

This front-loaded time is non-negotiable. A soup that boils for an hour without that foundation never catches up to one that had a proper base built in the first 15 minutes.

Dried vs. canned beans

  • Canned beans are the weeknight default. Drain and rinse to remove the aquafaba, which can make the soup slightly gluey. One 400g can replaces about 120g dried.
  • Dried beans are cheaper, have noticeably better texture, and produce more flavorful cooking liquid that contributes body to the soup. They need a soak (overnight or 1-hour quick-soak with boiling water) and about 60–90 minutes cooking before the soup is built.
  • Chickpeas specifically hold their shape through long simmers better than any other bean — they're the go-to for soups meant to reheat for several days.

Finishing techniques that separate good from great

Every soup recipe in this collection ends with at least one of four finishing moves, and they're worth learning because they translate to every soup you'll ever make:

Acid: a squeeze of lemon or a splash of sherry vinegar stirred in off the heat. Soups develop rounded, heavy flavors during long cooking, and a small amount of acid at the end brings everything into focus. Even tomato-based soups benefit from a tablespoon of lemon juice in the final stir.

Fresh herbs: parsley, cilantro, dill, or basil chopped and stirred in or scattered on top. Dried herbs added at the start contribute base notes; fresh herbs at the end contribute top notes. Great soups have both.

A finishing fat: a drizzle of good olive oil, a knob of butter stirred in, or a spoonful of yogurt or sour cream on top. Fat carries flavor and adds the glossy finish that distinguishes restaurant soups from weeknight ones.

A crunchy element: toasted bread, crushed tortilla chips, fried shallots, pumpkin seeds, or crumbled bacon. Textural contrast is what makes a bowl feel like a complete meal rather than a side.

The ten soups below cover everything from 25-minute weeknight saves (tomato basil, coconut lentil) to slower Sunday cooks (Tuscan white bean, chickpea spinach stew). Start with the coconut lentil if you're new to bean-based soups — it's forgiving, fast, and demonstrates the build-your-flavor-first principle clearly.

Frequently asked questions

Can I make these soups in a slow cooker or Instant Pot?
Most of them, yes. For slow cooker: brown the aromatics on the stove first (non-negotiable for flavor), then transfer everything to the slow cooker for 6–8 hours on low. For Instant Pot: use the sauté function to build the base, then pressure cook for 15–25 minutes depending on whether the recipe uses dried beans. The cream-based soups (coconut lentil, Tuscan white bean) should have the dairy added after pressure cooking, not during.
How long do one-pot soups keep in the fridge?
Bean and vegetable soups keep for 5 days refrigerated — they often taste better on days 2 and 3 as flavors meld. Chicken or seafood soups are best within 3 days. For longer storage, freeze in portions for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge and warm gently on the stove. Avoid the microwave for brothy soups — stove reheating preserves texture better.
Why is my bean soup thick and pasty instead of brothy?
Either too few beans or overcooking. Canned beans that simmer for over 40 minutes start to break down and release starch that thickens the broth into a paste. Add them in the last 15–20 minutes of cooking, not at the start. If you're already pasty, add 200–300 ml of additional broth and a squeeze of lemon to thin it back out.
Can I make these soups vegetarian?
Most are vegetarian already or can be made so by swapping vegetable broth for chicken broth and omitting meat. The chicken tortilla, taco, and lasagna soups specifically rely on meat for body — substitute with an extra can of beans and a tablespoon of tomato paste to compensate for the missing richness.
How do I thicken a soup without cornstarch?
Three natural methods: blend a cup of the soup with an immersion blender and stir it back in (works for bean and vegetable soups); mash a cup of the cooked beans with a fork against the side of the pot (same effect, less equipment); or reduce the soup by simmering uncovered for 10–15 minutes at the end. All three produce silkier, more integrated thickening than cornstarch slurry.
What's the best pot for making soup?
A 5–6 quart Dutch oven or heavy-bottomed stockpot. Enameled cast iron (Le Creuset, Lodge) holds heat evenly and handles long simmers without scorching. Thin stainless steel stockpots work but need more attention to prevent sticking at the bottom. Avoid non-stick for soups — the long simmers can degrade the coating.
